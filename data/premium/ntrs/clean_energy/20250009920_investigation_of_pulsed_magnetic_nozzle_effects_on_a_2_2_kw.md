# Investigation of Pulsed Magnetic Nozzle Effects on a 2.2 kW Inductively Coupled Argon Plasma Jet Using Fast Electrostatic and Magnetic Diagnostics

## Summary

Investigation of Pulsed Magnetic Nozzle Effects on a 2.2 kW Inductively Coupled Argon Plasma Jet Using Fast Electrostatic and Magnetic Diagnostics

## Abstract

Introduction and Motivation

Magnetic nozzles (MNs) offer a contactless means of accelerating and shaping plasma exhausts through applied magnetic fields, enabling efficient momentum transfer without physical confinement surfaces. In electric propulsion systems, they serve as magnetic analogues to gas-dynamic nozzles, while in high-enthalpy facilities they provide insight into plasma–field coupling relevant to reentry flows and astrophysical jet analogues.

Despite extensive theoretical modeling, there remains a lack of experimental data examining transient or pulsed magnetic nozzles operating in low-ionization, collisional plasma regimes such as those produced in inductively coupled plasma (ICP) jets. This work presents the design, implementation, and characterization of a pulsed magnetic nozzle integrated into the ARCTRON facility — a 2.2 kW ICP experiment developed at NASA Ames Research Center.

The study leverages a suite of fast-sweeping Langmuir probes, B-dot magnetic probes, and fast imaging diagnostics to examine how rapidly varying magnetic fields influence plasma acceleration, confinement, and flow geometry. The overarching goals are to (1) quantify the transient plasma response to pulsed magnetic fields, (2) demonstrate the utility of compact, high-frequency electrostatic diagnostics in magnetized, high-EMI environments, and (3) inform the development of magnetically assisted plasma thrusters and facility testbeds.
