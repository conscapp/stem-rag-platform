# What was claimed

In March 1989, electrochemists Martin Fleischmann and Stanley Pons reported excess heat and neutron signatures from a palladium-deuterium electrolytic cell at the University of Utah. The claim implied deuterium-deuterium fusion at near room temperature without the plasma confinement used in tokamaks.

# What independent replication found

Major laboratories (including MIT, Caltech, Harwell, CERN) attempted replication under controlled conditions. Most groups reported **no reproducible excess heat** beyond calibration uncertainty and **no neutron flux** at levels consistent with claimed fusion rates. The original neutron measurements were later attributed to instrumental artifacts in several replications.

# Lessons for innovators (not a verdict on future LENR)

1. **Calibration dominates**: small systematic errors in calorimetry can mimic "excess heat" at the watt scale.
2. **Replication protocol matters**: undisclosed electrode preparation, loading ratios, and isotope purity strongly affect outcomes.
3. **Signal-to-noise for rare events**: claimed fusion rates would produce detectable radiation; null radiation measurements constrain mechanisms.
4. **Publication pressure**: high-visibility announcements before peer review reduced community trust and slowed structured replication.

# What the literature still documents

A minority of groups continue LENR research with heterogeneous results; mainstream plasma-fusion programs (tokamaks, inertial confinement) remain the primary funded path with published confinement scaling laws (Lawson criterion, triple product).

# Open questions an agent should surface

- What specific calorimeter design would isolate chemical vs nuclear heat in a Pd-D cell?
- Which isotope loading fraction in palladium is required for any anomalous thermal signal?
- What peer-reviewed null-result papers exist for each claimed LENR mechanism?
