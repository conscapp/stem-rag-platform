# Context

The National Ignition Facility (NIF) uses inertial confinement fusion: 192 laser beams compress a deuterium-tritium fuel capsule inside a hohlraum. The goal is ignition — fusion energy output exceeding the energy delivered to the fuel.

# Documented shortfall (pre-2022)

For many years NIF shots achieved fusion yields below ignition thresholds predicted by early radiation-hydrodynamics models. Key gaps identified in open literature:

- **Asymmetry**: capsule implosion non-uniformity reduced compression density.
- **Mixing**: ablator material mixed into the hot spot cooled the plasma.
- **Laser-plasma instabilities**: energy coupling to the hohlraum differed from design codes.

# Lessons learned

1. **Integrated diagnostics**: high-resolution X-ray imaging revealed failure modes invisible to yield alone.
2. **Model validation loop**: each failed shot updated boundary conditions for simulation — negative results accelerated code maturity.
3. **Q-factor vs ignition**: energy gain definitions must separate driver energy, capsule energy, and total facility energy.

# Later documented progress

In December 2022 NIF reported a shot exceeding fusion energy yield relative to laser energy deposited in the fuel (scientific breakeven for the fuel). This does not imply commercial reactor readiness; engineering Q and repetition rate remain open.
