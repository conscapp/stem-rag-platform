# Context

The ITER divertor must exhaust extreme heat fluxes from the scrape-off layer while surviving neutron damage and tritium retention constraints. Early design assumptions for peak heat flux (~10 MW/m² steady, higher transients) have been repeatedly revised as scrape-off-layer width measurements from existing tokamaks suggested narrower, hotter footprints.

# Documented gaps

1. **Scrape-off-layer width uncertainty**: empirical scaling from current devices may not extrapolate to ITER plasma current.
2. **Transient loads**: edge-localized modes (ELMs) can deposit energy faster than tungsten monoblock cooling can remove it without melting risk.
3. **Tritium inventory**: co-deposited layers in divertor tiles complicate safety and licensing budgets.
4. **Material choice tradeoffs**: tungsten resists sputtering but is brittle under neutron irradiation; carbon alternatives raise tritium retention.

# Lessons for innovators

- Negative or inconclusive divertor tests are information: they bound allowable ELM energy and required detachment regimes.
- Detachment physics (radiative divertor) is as critical as core confinement for a reactor path.
- "Missing information" often means missing multi-machine scaling data, not a single failed shot.
