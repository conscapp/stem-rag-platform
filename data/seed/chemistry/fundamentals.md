# Chemical Fundamentals

## Atomic Structure

Atoms consist of protons, neutrons (nucleus), and electrons. Atomic number $Z$ = number of protons.
Mass number $A$ = protons + neutrons. Isotopes share $Z$ but differ in $A$ (e.g. $^1\mathrm{H}$,
$^2\mathrm{H}$ deuterium, $^3\mathrm{H}$ tritium — central to fusion fuel cycles).

Quantum numbers $(n,\ell,m_s)$ organize electron configurations; Aufbau, Hund, and Pauli rules
build the periodic table. Effective nuclear charge and shielding explain periodic trends.

## Periodic Trends

Electronegativity and ionization energy generally increase across a period and decrease down a group.
Atomic radius trends opposite. These trends govern ionic vs covalent bonding preferences and acid–base
character of oxides.

## Chemical Bonding

- **Ionic:** electron transfer; lattice energy from Coulomb attraction in crystals (Born–Haber cycle).
- **Covalent:** shared electron pairs; bond order correlates with strength and length; polarity from
  electronegativity differences.
- **Metallic:** delocalized electrons; explains conductivity and malleability.
- **Intermolecular:** hydrogen bonding, dipole–dipole, London dispersion — control boiling points
  and solubility (like dissolves like).

Molecular orbital and band theory extend bonding to conjugated molecules and solids (semiconductors,
graphene) relevant to nanotechnology.

## Stoichiometry

Moles: $n = m/M$. Concentration: $C = n/V$ (mol/L). Dilution: $C_1 V_1 = C_2 V_2$.

Limiting reagent determines theoretical yield; percent yield $=(\mathrm{actual}/\mathrm{theoretical})\times 100\%$.
Gas stoichiometry uses $PV=nRT$ at moderate conditions.

## Thermochemistry

Enthalpy: $\Delta H = H_{\mathrm{products}} - H_{\mathrm{reactants}}$. Exothermic $\Delta H<0$.

**Hess's law:** $\Delta H$ is path-independent (state function): $\Delta H_{\mathrm{total}}=\sum\Delta H_{\mathrm{steps}}$.

Standard enthalpies of formation $\Delta H_f^\circ$ tabulate formation from elements; reaction enthalpies
follow $\sum \nu\Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{products})-\sum\nu\Delta H_f^\circ(\mathrm{reactants})$.

**Gibbs free energy:** $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$. Spontaneous (at constant $T,P$) when
$\Delta G<0$. Relation to equilibrium: $\Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K$.

## Equilibrium

For $aA + bB \rightleftharpoons cC + dD$:
$$K_{eq} = \frac{[C]^c [D]^d}{[A]^a [B]^b}$$
(activities in rigorous form). Reaction quotient $Q$ uses instantaneous concentrations; $Q<K$ drives
forward. Le Chatelier: system shifts to counteract imposed stress (concentration, pressure for gases,
temperature — which also changes $K$ via van 't Hoff).

## Acids and Bases

$pH=-\log[H^+]$, $pOH=-\log[OH^-]$, $pH+pOH=14$ at 25°C (from $K_w=10^{-14}$).

Weak acid: $K_a=[H^+][A^-]/[HA]$; $pK_a=-\log K_a$. Buffers: Henderson–Hasselbalch
$pH=pK_a+\log([A^-]/[HA])$.

## Reaction Kinetics

Rate $=k[A]^m[B]^n$ (orders from experiment, not stoichiometry in general).
**Arrhenius:** $k=A e^{-E_a/RT}$. Catalysts lower $E_a$ via alternate pathways; enzymes and
heterogeneous catalysts are engineering-critical.

Half-life for first-order: $t_{1/2}=\ln 2/k$. Mechanisms (elementary steps) must sum to overall
reaction and be consistent with observed rate laws (rate-determining step, steady-state approximations).

## Electrochemistry

Oxidation/reduction (redox); galvanic cells convert chemical to electrical energy.
**Nernst:** $E=E^\circ-\frac{RT}{nF}\ln Q$. Faraday constant $F\approx 96485\,\mathrm{C/mol}$.
Batteries, fuel cells, and electrolysis are the applied face of these relations — including
energy-storage and hydrogen production for clean-energy systems.
