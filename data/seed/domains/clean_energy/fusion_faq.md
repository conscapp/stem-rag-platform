# Fusion FAQ: Isotopes & Room-Temperature Questions

## Hydrogen Isotopes for Fusion

Hydrogen has three naturally relevant isotopes for fusion research [hydrogen_isotopes]:

| Isotope | Name | Nucleus | Notes |
|---------|------|---------|-------|
| $^1H$ | protium (ordinary hydrogen) | 1 proton | Most abundant; harder to fuse at low energy |
| $^2H$ | deuterium (D) | proton + neutron | Used in D–T and D–D reactions |
| $^3H$ | tritium (T) | proton + 2 neutrons | Radioactive; bred from lithium in reactor blankets |

**D–T fusion** ($^2H + ^3H \rightarrow ^4He + n + 17.6\ \text{MeV}$) has the lowest ignition temperature among common fuel choices and is the basis for ITER and most tokamak roadmaps [nuclear_fusion].

**Other fuel cycles** exist in research literature:
- **D–D fusion**: both deuterium; requires higher temperature than D–T
- **D–$^3He$ fusion**: aneutronic (fewer neutrons); $^3He$ is rare on Earth
- **p–$^{11}B$ (proton-boron)**: aneutronic; requires very high plasma temperatures

Choosing an isotope trades reaction cross-section, neutron production, fuel availability, and required plasma conditions [nuclear_fusion + plasma_physics].

## What "Room Temperature" Means in Fusion Context

Everyday "room temperature" is roughly $300\ \text{K}$ ($27°\text{C}$).

**Hot fusion** (tokamaks, stellarators, inertial confinement) requires plasma temperatures on the order of $10^7$–$10^8\ \text{K}$ so nuclei overcome Coulomb repulsion [plasma_physics]. The reactor vessel is at room temperature; the fuel inside is not.

**Low-temperature fusion research** (sometimes called cold fusion or LENR in historical literature) refers to claims of nuclear reactions near ambient conditions. Mainstream plasma physics knowledge in our corpus describes hot fusion conditions; low-temperature approaches require their own experimental evidence base [cold_fusion].

To explore room-temperature nuclear energy paths, retrieval needs data on: specific reaction mechanisms proposed, energy balance, and reproducible measurements — not just plasma temperature targets [nuclear_fusion].

## Connecting Isotope Choice to Energy Balance

Mass-energy conservation: $E = \Delta m c^2$ [conservation_energy].

Each fuel cycle releases a characteristic energy per reaction. Plant output also depends on:
- Confinement quality ($n \tau_E T$ Lawson triple product) [plasma_physics]
- Energy gain $Q = P_{\text{fusion}} / P_{\text{input}}$ [nuclear_fusion]
- Thermal conversion efficiency (Carnot limit) [thermodynamics]
